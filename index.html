Overview: Climate change, primarily driven by greenhouse gas emissions, has already started to noticably impact our world, and there is no sign of it slowing down in the near future. We aim to investigate both the causes and the effects of this pressing global issue through our narrative.

The industrial age marked a new era — not just for humans with their technological advancements, but also for Earth itself with the start of environmental pollution at scale. The release of toxic greenhouse gases into the atmosphere has affected all aspects of the biosphere. In recent years, we have been made increasingly aware of these effects on our day-to-day lives. Global temperatures have soared to new highs and we have seen unprecedented weather activity around the world.

This shift in our environment is referred to as climate change, and is considered to be humanity's greatest threat. Yet, as big of an issue as climate change is regarded to be, there are those who do not believe in its effects on our planet. In a study conducted by Yale University in 2021, ~71.8% of Americans believed in climate change, while ~14.5% of American did not believe that climate change was real.

This lack of consensus has made climate change a politically charged topic, and thus one that humanity has been very slow to act upon due to modern bureaucracy.

Despite those who do not believe in climate change, data collected by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) has showed an increase in storm frequency since 1955.

Alongside any material and environmental damage of more violent storms, increased global temperatures have led to the destruction of critical environments such as the Great Barrier Reef in Australia.

Given the historical data that we now possess, we can establish that climate change is a real issue that has real effects. How did it become such a large problem, and why has it not been solved yet?

A cause of the climate change is the climbing global temperatures as a result in increased amounts of greenhouse gases in our atmosphere. These greenhouse gases trap light from the sun in our atmosphere, resulting in the gasees trapping heat as well, causing the warming affect we see in the global rising temperatures.

The primary greenhouse gas emitted by humans is CO₂, this being that our breath results in the production of CO₂ as well the burning of fossil fuels for energy production and transportation. This results in CO₂ accounting for almost 80% of the U.S. greenhouse gas emissions in 2020.

Carbon dioxide makes up the overwhelming majority of greenhouse gas emissions (about 79%), but it is not the only greenhouse gas that we need to worry about. Other gasses, such as methane and nitrous oxide, are also large contributors to climate change.

Methane, which is produced during the production and transport of coal, natural gas, and oil, currently accounts for about 11% of greenhouse gas emissions in the United States. It is also emitted by livestock and from other farming practices as well as from the decomposition of organic waste, such as in landfills. While this gas is not nearly as prevalent as carbon dioxide, it has a higher Global Warming Potential (GWP) of 27, which means that it absorbs roughly 27 times more gas over time than an equivalent amount of carbon dioxide, and therefore also contributes more to global warming than its relatively small percentage of total emissions might suggest.

Nitrous oxide is a byproduct of many industrial and agricultural processes, as well as from burning fossil fuels, and makes up around 7% of the total greenhouse gas emissions in the U.S. Its GWP is even higher than methane, causing it to absorb around 273 times as much gas as an equivalent amount of carbon dioxide. Because of this, while carbon dioxide is certainly an important gas to try to reduce emissions of, it is also important to try and reduce the emissions of other greenhouse gasses that can be just as damaging to the environment.

Unfortunately, reducing greenhouse gas emissions is not as simple as telling countries to outlaw harmful processes. The graph on the left charts countries by their Human Development Index (HDI). For many undeveloped countries, nonrenewable energy sources are their only reliable way of powering the grid, and they may not have the financial capacity to focus on renewable energy.

On the other end of the spectrum, developed countries tend to have a large population of citizens, and thus a large demand for energy. Because renewable sources of energy are intermittent (and do not run continuously), satisfying 100% of the energy demand is not feasible with current technology, and thus a reliance also exists on nonrenewable sources to fill the gap.

While countries may grow in population linearly, their energy and resource demands increase exponentially. The modern infrastructure and supply chain does not exist to enable renewable energy as the sole provider of energy going forwards, so currently supply falls back on nonrenewable energy to fill in the quota. Thus, large countries face the problem of having to replace a deeply-baked (and continuously growing) nonrenewable power grid with a renewable solution, while the cost of replacement grows exponentially with the size and demand of the grid.

Energy demand is not the only contributing factor to increased emissions in large countries. The widespread usage of concrete in developed countries has led to cement being one of the largest contributors to global CO2 emissions. Additionally, increase in material demand has created an economic reliance on factories which release harmful and toxic gases as part of the manufacturing process.

Climate change cannot be solved with a "eureka" moment, it is a complex and challenging societal issue that will take decades of fighting to overcome. As the effects of climate change become more impactful on our day-to-day lives, more global attention will be focused on the issue (and thus more resources will be committed towards working on a solution). In the years to come, renewable energy sources such as nuclear fusion may be proven to be viable, and global restrictions could be set in place to cap the amount of regulated emissions per country.
